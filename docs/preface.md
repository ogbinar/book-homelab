# Preface: How to Use This Book

This book is written for readers who want a homelab that is practical, affordable, and sustainable. Hindi ito para sa setup na maganda lang sa picture pero mahirap panindigan after two weeks.

The goal is simple: start with what you already have, learn the core patterns well, and build only what you can keep running.

The book uses a school ladder to show maturity, but it keeps the real-world metaphor in the house. Think power, locks, front desk, water reserve, game room, and study room. If a topic does not fit a real household need, it probably belongs later or outside the core path.

---

## Read This Book in Order First

For your first pass, read the chapters in sequence.

- **Part I** gets you from zero to a working setup.
- **Part II** helps you make that setup more reliable, secure, and worth documenting.
- **The appendices** are reference material. Use them when you need a shortcut, a checklist, or a clearer definition.

If you already have some experience, puwede kang lumaktaw. But if this is your first real server, the cleanest path is still the boring one: one chapter at a time.

---

## Build for Your Actual Life

A good homelab should fit your real conditions.

- If your budget is tight, reused or second-hand hardware is a valid starting point.
- If your area gets brownouts, plan for graceful shutdowns and backups early.
- If your internet is shared with family, housemates, or work-from-home traffic, keep your setup light and predictable.
- If your ISP is PLDT, Globe, or Converge and you are behind CGNAT, that is normal. The book accounts for that reality.
- If a service is just for you, keep it private. If it is for the family, keep it simple. If it is public, treat it like a front gate.

You do not need a rack, enterprise gear, or a dedicated room. Sa maraming bahay, an old laptop, a used mini PC, or one quiet box beside the router is already enough.

---

## What “Success” Looks Like Here

Success in this book does not mean running the biggest stack.

It means:

- you understand why each service exists;
- you can recover when something breaks;
- you know the limits of your hardware and connection; and
- your setup still makes sense for your budget, kuryente bill, and daily routine.

That includes knowing who the service is really for: self, family, or the public.

That is already real systems work.

---

## Use the Community Properly

You will learn faster if you do not build in isolation. Ask questions. Compare notes. Share small wins. Filipino tech communities are often strongest when people are direct, generous, and practical.

Bring specifics when you ask for help:

- what hardware you are using;
- which ISP you are on;
- whether the problem is local network, internet access, storage, or power; and
- what you already tried.

That makes it easier for other people to help you well.

---

## Before You Continue

Keep three expectations in mind:

1. **Start small.** One working service is better than five half-working ones.
2. **Prefer maintainable choices.** Cheap hardware is fine; fragile habits are not.
3. **Document as you go.** Future-you will forget more than you think.

If that sounds acceptable, you are ready.
