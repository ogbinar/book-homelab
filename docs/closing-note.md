# Closing Note

If you reached this page, you already did more than most people who say they want to learn infrastructure.

You chose hardware. You installed systems. You broke things, fixed things, and learned how small decisions affect reliability, cost, and peace of mind. That matters.

This book argued for a simple idea: a homelab should serve your life, not consume it.

That principle matters even more in the Philippine context. Many readers are starting with second-hand hardware, shared Wi-Fi, limited budget, uneven power, and ISP constraints like PLDT, Globe, Converge, or CGNAT. Those are not side notes. They are part of the design brief.

So as you continue, keep the standard realistic:

- choose hardware you can replace or repair;
- keep backups more seriously than aesthetics;
- design for interruptions, including brownouts and router resets; and
- upgrade only when the next step solves a real problem.

A small lab that survives ordinary life is better than a flashy one that only works on a good week.

You also do not need to learn alone. Maraming Pilipino na natututo through shared notes, second-hand recommendations, Discord threads, FB Marketplace finds, and patient community help. Bring that same generosity when your turn comes.

If this book did its job, you now have more than a box running services at home. You have a working practice: observe, document, simplify, recover, repeat.

That is enough to keep building.
